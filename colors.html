Colors

D8A runs monochrome with one weapon. Black for authority. Gray for hierarchy. White for space. Redline for the point — the red pen that cuts the excuse, drawn exactly once per surface.

Redline — the accent

Redline is the engineer's red pen — the mark on a spec that says this stays, that goes — and the line an engine runs at. It's blunt on purpose: red has been the color of revolutions and of classic design for a century, from constructivist posters to Swiss typography. It marks the CTA, the key metric, the hero chart series, the strike through the excuse. Never decoration, never an error state.

Bridge tones

The gap between the grays and the red is bridged by three in-between tones. Be honest about what two of them are: Red 900 and Ink Red are tinted blacks. In a small chip they read as near-black — that's by design. Their job is done at full-canvas scale, where the undertone warms the surface without ever reading as “a red background”. If a surface obviously looks red, the tint is too strong.

Gradients & glow

Four gradient recipes, and only these four. Every gradient stays inside one hue family — gray or red — or fades to transparent. Never two hues, never a rainbow.

Signal Glow
radial · red 16% → 0%
Ambient light behind hero content on dark. Atmosphere, not a statement
Canvas Depth
#0a0a0a → #241014
Default dark canvas — black warming into Ink Red
Red Ramp
#ff2b36 → #e4002b
CTA fills and hero elements — red with depth, within the family
Red Fade
red 25% → transparent
Area-chart fills, pulse tails, section transitions
  • One family per gradient. Gray→gray, red→red, or red→transparent. A gradient is depth, not a second color.
  • Glow is ambient light, not an effect. Radial, under 20% peak opacity, behind content — never a drop shadow, never a colored border glow on cards.
  • Display headlines on dark may run White → Flare (see Fonts). Body text never gradients.
  • Still banned: gloss, bevels, multi-hue gradients, gradients on light canvas backgrounds (light stays clean — depth is a dark-canvas tool).

The one-weapon rule

  • One red statement per surface. A slide, a section, a social image, a chart — each gets exactly one red element. The CTA, or the number, or the hero series. Never several.
  • Statement vs. atmosphere. The rule counts statements — CTAs, metrics, hero series, the pulse. Atmosphere — the Signal Glow, bridge-tone surfaces, fades under 25% opacity — doesn't count. Atmosphere sets the stage; the statement makes the point.
  • Red marks the point, not the theme. If you can remove the red and the message survives, it was in the wrong place.
  • If everything is red, nothing is. When in doubt, take red away — the monochrome system works without it.
Do
A dark slide, gray body text, one red metric: “3× faster”

The red does one job: it points at the proof.

Don't
Red headline, red icons, red borders, red background section

Four red elements, zero emphasis. The weapon only works drawn once.

Dark-led dual system

The brand leads on black — but not flat black. Marketing surfaces sit on Canvas Depth (#0a0a0a warming into Ink Red), with the Signal Glow as ambient light behind hero content. The light canvas is for reading: docs, email, long-form — and stays clean, no gradients. Same system, same rules, different room.

Color pairing rules

  • Text on light backgrounds: Use Black (#0a0a0a) or Gray 600 (#525252) for body. Never lighter than Gray 500.
  • Text on dark backgrounds: Use White (#fafafa) or Gray 300 (#d4d4d4) for body. Never darker than Gray 400.
  • Borders: Gray 200 on light surfaces, Gray 800 on dark surfaces.
  • Emphasis: Hierarchy comes from weight, size, and contrast. Red is reserved for the single point of the surface — not for general emphasis.
  • Red on dark: Redline (#ff2b36) only. It's tuned for the black canvas — large marks, strikes, and UI, not paragraphs.
  • Red on light: Red 600 (#e4002b) for fills, text, and links (4.7:1 AA), Red 700 (#b3001f) for small text and hovers. Redline itself never carries body text on white (3.6:1 — UI and large type only).
  • Never red-on-red, red text on mid-grays, or red paired with any other hue.
  • Red is the mark, not an error state. Where an interface or chart encodes pass/fail semantics, the brand red steps aside — don't make the reader guess whether red means "the point" or "broken".

Accessibility

  • Black on White: 19.3:1 contrast ratio — WCAG AAA
  • Gray 600 on White: 7.1:1 contrast ratio — WCAG AAA
  • Gray 500 on White: 4.6:1 contrast ratio — WCAG AA (large text only)
  • White on Gray 950: 16.2:1 contrast ratio — WCAG AAA
  • Redline on Black: 5.3:1 contrast ratio — WCAG AA. Red carries marks and headlines, not paragraphs
  • Red 600 on White: 4.7:1 contrast ratio — WCAG AA body text
  • Red 700 on White: 6.3:1 contrast ratio — WCAG AA+
  • Redline on White: 3.6:1 — UI components and large type only. Never body text.

Why Redline

v2.0 tried CI green — the color of a passing pipeline. Correct, safe, and too nice. D8A's voice picks fights with the status quo; the color has to match. Red is the only accent that is both revolutionary and classic: the constructivist poster, the Swiss grid, the editor's pen, the engine at redline. In our world a “redline” is literally the markup an engineer puts on a spec — and D8A is spec-first. One caution shaped the shade: Databricks lives at orange-red (#ff3621), so Redline runs cooler and deeper, toward crimson — and appears as a mark (a strike, a line, a pulse), never as a logo-style color wash.
