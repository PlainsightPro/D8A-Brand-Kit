Photography

D8A uses three visual moods. Pick the right one for the context. Never mix moods on the same surface.

The three moods

Abstract & geometric

Data-inspired visuals. Grid patterns, flowing lines, node-and-connection diagrams. Used for hero sections, backgrounds, and brand-level storytelling. Think: the beauty of data structure made visual.

Dark moody tech

High-contrast, low-light, dark backgrounds with subtle illumination. Server racks under status LEDs, circuit-board close-ups, code on screens in the dark. Used for technical content, developer-facing materials, and "under the hood" stories.

Clean, bright, minimal

White space, sharp edges, no clutter. Product screenshots, UI details, workspace setups. Used for product marketing, feature pages, and documentation. The product is the hero — the photo just frames it.

Photo treatment

  • Desaturate. Reduce saturation to 60–80% for all photography. D8A is monochrome — photos should feel tonal, not colorful.
  • No filters. No Instagram-style overlays, no color grading effects, no vintage treatments.
  • No stock clichés. No handshakes, no people pointing at screens, no "diverse team laughing in a glass meeting room."
  • Crop tight. Remove anything that doesn't serve the message. Negative space is intentional, not accidental.
  • Monochrome conversion is always safe. If unsure about color treatment, convert to black and white. It always works with the D8A palette.
  • Red is light, not grade. In dark-moody shots, ambient red illumination is on-brand — status LEDs, warning strips, darkroom red as light sources in the frame. But never color-grade a whole image toward red: the accent should read as light falling on the scene, not a filter over it.

Do / Don't

Do
Abstract data visualizations — flowing lines, grid structures, node graphs rendered as art
Don't
Generic stock photos of "data" — blue glowing globes, matrix-style falling numbers, stock dashboards
Do
Real product screenshots showing the actual D8A interface in use
Don't
Mockups of imaginary interfaces or photoshopped screen content
Do
Dark, moody close-ups of real technology — cables, servers, screens with code
Don't
Overly staged "tech" photos with perfect lighting and models pretending to code
