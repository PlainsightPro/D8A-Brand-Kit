07 · Brand voice

How we sound

D8A speaks like a sharp colleague, not a marketing department. Direct. Positive. Backed by evidence. Zero fluff.

Three principles

01

Direct, not aggressive

Say what you mean in as few words as possible. Cut the preamble. But don't punch down — D8A is confident, not condescending. We challenge the status quo, not the reader.

02

Positive, not preachy

Frame everything around what teams can achieve, not what they're doing wrong. "Ship more and better" beats "stop wasting time." Motivate, don't lecture.

03

Prove it, don't proclaim it

Every claim needs a receipt. Show a number, a screenshot, a before/after. If you can't back it up, don't say it. D8A earns credibility through demonstration.

We say / we don't say

We say
"Ship data, not excuses."
Short, active, confrontational in a positive way. Sets the tone immediately.
We don't say
"Empower your data teams to unlock synergies across the enterprise."
Meaningless corporate filler. Every word is a buzzword. Says nothing specific.
We say
"Connect your stack. Write a spec. Get a pipeline. That's it."
Concrete steps. The reader can picture doing this. No abstraction.
We don't say
"We integrate seamlessly with your existing data ecosystem."
"Seamlessly" is a red flag. "Data ecosystem" is vague. This could describe any product.
We say
"Your data team could ship 3x more pipelines this quarter."
Specific, positive, forward-looking. Focuses on what's possible, not what's broken.
We don't say
"Your data team is spending 80% of their time on stuff that doesn't ship."
True, but negative. Makes the reader feel attacked rather than inspired. Reframe as opportunity.
We say
"Platform-agnostic DataOps."
Three words. Immediately positions D8A. Technical audience gets it instantly.
We don't say
"A comprehensive, end-to-end data operations management solution."
Word soup. "Comprehensive" and "end-to-end" are padding. "Solution" is the most overused word in enterprise tech.

Tone

We sound like

  • A sharp technical colleague who cuts through noise
  • A product demo that speaks for itself
  • A well-written README — clear, structured, useful

We avoid sounding like

  • A marketing brochure full of superlatives
  • A consultant slide deck with more buzzwords than ideas
  • A press release that says nothing in 400 words

LinkedIn & social content

  • Open with a punch. The first line decides whether anyone reads the rest. Lead with a bold claim, a surprising number, or a direct challenge.
  • One idea per post. Don't try to cover everything. Go deep on one thing.
  • Show the product. Screenshots, GIFs, short videos. The product is the proof.
  • End with a clear next step. Not "thoughts?" — something specific. A link, a question about their stack, an invitation to try.
  • No hashtag spam. Two hashtags maximum. Three if one is #D8A.
  • Alternate topics. Don't post about the same feature three times in a row. Rotate: product, opinion, customer story, technical insight.

Language rules

  • English only. All external communication is in English.
  • Sentence case. For headings, buttons, labels. Not Title Case, not ALL CAPS (except the D8A wordmark).
  • One thought per sentence. If it needs a semicolon, split it.
  • No emoji in body copy. Emoji are allowed in social media captions and casual Slack-style content. Never in product copy, docs, or formal communications.
  • Spell out acronyms once. First mention: "DataOps (Data Operations)." After that: "DataOps."
  • Active voice. "D8A generates the spec" not "The spec is generated by D8A."
